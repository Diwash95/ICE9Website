Task 1: Scatter Plot Analysis

[Image Placeholder: Scatter Plot (NY vs LA)]

The main goal of this chart was to see how the inflation index for Los Angeles and New York has changed over time. What you can easily notice is that **New York always has a higher inflation index than Los Angeles** for every year shown. Task 2: Stacked Bar Chart Analysis

[Image Placeholder: Stacked Bar Chart (2018+)]

This chart focuses on the inflation index for the last few years, starting from 2018. Since it is a stacked bar chart, the height of each bar shows the **total combined inflation index** of all four regions (US Average, NY, LA, and Chicago) for that year. The biggest takeaway here is the overall trend: the total height of the bars gets noticeably larger as you move from 2018 to 2022. This shows that the combined inflation pressure across these major US areas was consistently growing, especially with a visible jump in 2022. The colors inside the bar also tell us that New York is consistently the biggest contributor to that yearly total. 

                            
                            # Snippet from data_analysis.py for Task 2:
                            bar_chart = alt.Chart(df_recent).mark_bar().encode(
                                x=alt.X('Year:O', title='Year'),
                                y=alt.Y('InflationIndex:Q', stack='zero', ...),
                                color=alt.Color('City:N', title='Metropolitan Area')
                            ).properties(title='Stacked Bar Chart ...')

Task 3: Stacked Area Chart Analysis

[Image Placeholder: Stacked Area Chart (All Years)]

The area chart is helpful for seeing the change in the total inflation index over the entire time span (starting from 2000). The overall look of the chart is a smooth, continuous rise from the left to the right. The entire colored area is getting bigger as time goes on, which confirms the **long-term upward trend of the inflation index** across all regions. New York's section takes up the most area, simply because it generally has the largest index value each year, making it the highest line on the chart. This chart emphasizes the idea of a continuous, compounding increase in inflation over two decades.

Task 4: Line Chart Analysis

[Image Placeholder: Line Chart (All Regions)]

The line chart is the clearest way to compare how the inflation index has moved for the four different regions over all the years in the dataset. All four lines follow a **very similar path**, moving upwards together over time, which means they are all affected by the same larger economic changes. You can see the clear ranking of the cities: the New York line is always on top (highest inflation), followed by Los Angeles, then Chicago, and the US City Average usually sits slightly below or sometimes crossing over the Chicago line. This visual separation of the lines makes it very easy to track the individual progress of each city and see that their relative ranking for inflation has remained quite stable. 

                            
                            # Snippet from data_analysis.py for Task 4:
                            line_chart = alt.Chart(df_all_long).mark_line().encode(
                                x=alt.X('Year:O', title='Year'),
                                y=alt.Y('InflationIndex:Q', title='Inflation Index Value'),
                                color=alt.Color('City:N', title='Metropolitan Area')
                            ).properties(title='Line Chart Showing Inflation Index ...')
